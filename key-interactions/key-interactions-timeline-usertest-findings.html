What to do next

Ship with confidence

  • The filterable timeline/list as the primary surface
  • Type filters, search, and date range as the control set
  • The Undated tab pattern
  • System-generated events included as baseline content

Iterate before shipping

  • Make unfiltered list scannable so filtering is often unnecessary (4/4, behavioral)
  • Make filter state legible at rest with active labels (4/4)
  • Make cards clickable with a slide-over for detail (4/4)
  • Add record-type label near page title to differentiate org and request (inferred)
  • Add evaluations and evaluator notes to taxonomy (unprompted)

Design and test in a follow-up round

  • Org-level and non-grant interactions: what can an interaction attach to (grant, org, contact, prospect)? This is the top scope question and should get its own design round ahead of card polish.
  • Park the visualization, don't cut it. Re-test against the eligibility-decision use case, likely nearer reporting than the record page.
  • Slide-over contents as a discrete question.

Data pulls to inform next steps

  • Org-level tracking prevalence: how many clients track interactions at the org level today? Sizes the org-level need.
  • Multi-grant-per-org prevalence: how many active orgs carry more than one grant? Sizes the request filter problem.
  • Tier placement: does interaction history belong in the base tier? Two unprompted asks suggest timeline-alone isn't an upsell driver.
Post-Ship Signals to Watch

Pre-ship research structurally cannot answer adoption; the disengaged majority isn't in any test we can run. Adoption breadth (how many accounts have any user logging), logging depth (interactions per active record, trended), behavior retention (do month-1 loggers log in month 3), and distribution (concentrated in power users or spread) are the signals that matter. Once a live population exists, a targeted qualitative study of low-logging accounts becomes the follow-on research priority.

The evidence behind the top findings

Confidence tag, synthesis, participant quotes where captured, and clip placeholders.

Convergent (strongest signal)

Interactions want to attach to more than grants. (One deep source, corroborated across prior research and design; centerpiece by consequence, not frequency.)

Both prototypes assume every interaction hangs off a grant, by construction. One participant articulated, repeatedly and in detail, that she needs to log interactions that don't: conversations with prospects before they're in the system, partnership and committee contacts, and advisory-council relationships tied to a person rather than a grant. A cold-recruited participant independently voiced a related instinct, that the grant-shaped record is too narrow and should show more of the relationship and decision history.

The sharpest framing (observed on video, paraphrased) was that relationship shape, grant frequency, recency, on-time materials, is an input to refunding eligibility, which makes org-level history a decision tool, not a log. This aligns with two prior signals outside this study: Dr. Scholl's Foundation already hacks manual grant entry to track interactions (EC), and Sammie has raised org-level interactions from the design side. Weight it as a high-consequence single-source finding with external corroboration, not a high-frequency one.

Convergent (4/4)

The list is how people actually work

All four preferred the list as their daily driver. The one visual-leaning participant who wanted both still named the list as how they actually work; the viz was an add-on they didn't want to lose, not a co-equal surface.

"Version 1 is how I work, but I like version 2's visual."

Convergent (4/4, behavioral)

Filtering is a recovery move, not the first move

Visible only on video, not in transcripts: all four scrolled to scan the list first, discovered more than one card could be the one they wanted, and only then reached for the filters to disambiguate. This relocates filtering in the workflow and raises the stakes on the unfiltered resting state: the more scannable the list, the less often people need to filter at all.

It also ties to card ambiguity and the scope-attribution problem: one card often doesn't say enough at a glance to rule it in or out.

Convergent (4/4)

Filter state isn't legible

Every participant, at some point, forgot a filter selection was still active and read the empty or short result as missing or broken data. In Version B this was worse: a stale search emptied the visualization while the count still showed results, and 2/4 read that contradiction as broken. At-rest filter labels need to state what's active.

"It says there's two, but I'm not seeing them... oh, it's because I had Sarah before."

Emerging, now convergent

The visualization doesn't drive navigation

1/4 preferred it; 0/4 used it to complete a find task. Its surviving use is at-a-glance cadence (how many in-person meetings this quarter), a different job than finding a moment. Honest caveat: Version B's stale-search bug was not fixed after session 1, so 2/4 judged the payments task against a partly-misfiring B. Some of B's poor showing is the defect, not the design.

Emerging (named unprompted)

Taxonomy gap: evaluations

At least one participant expected evaluation forms and evaluator notes as an interaction type and noted their absence. Cheap to add; answers a test-plan question directly.

Open question (centerpiece tension)

Is this a per-user log, or a team/org record?

The most important unresolved question heading into the next iteration, equivalent to the Request Summary readout's stage-adaptation tension. Signals pull in different directions: one participant framed it as a personal performance-review tracker; others framed its value as succession ("helpful for whoever takes over after me"); EC earlier argued pins should belong to the org, not the user.

Whether interactions, pins, and the record itself are scoped to a person, a team, or the organization is undecided, and it shapes the data model, the org-level attach-target work, and the eventual permissions story. Flag it as the thing to resolve first.

Reference

Study design

Unmoderated remote usability study conducted through Userlytics. 4 of 5 target participants completed the study. Participants were 1 handpicked SME-panel respondent, 2 cold Gainsight in-app recruits, and 1 product-team personal foundation contact. Each participant was asked to complete task sets across both Version A (date-range picker, filters, search) and Version B (swimlane visualization) in the same session.

Tasks traced back to the core research question: does a thoughtful, milestone-oriented visualization above the timeline add enough value to justify the complexity, or is the list with good filters and date controls enough?

Confidence thresholds (adjusted for n=4)

  • Convergent (3-4 of 4): Act now, with the sample caveat.
  • Emerging (2 of 4): Document and watch.
  • Isolated (1 of 4): Note, don't act.

Limitations

  • 4 of 5 target participants. The recruited sample was not the planned SME panel after handpicked recruitment stalled.
  • Selection bias toward engaged, feature-aware users. The disengaged majority, whose logging behavior determines adoption, is not represented. The recruitment difficulty is itself weak evidence of that gap.
  • Best-case data density. The seeded timeline (roughly 98 events, 4 years) is far richer than a typical client's, especially in human-logged events. This study cannot speak to value when relational logging is sparse.
  • Version B carried an unfixed bug after session 1: a stale search filter emptied the visualization while filter counts still showed results, which 2/4 read as missing data. Every B-vs-A judgment in this study was made against a partly-misfiring B.
  • No SUS or difficulty scales collected (time cap).
  • Role and segment analysis not possible at n=4.

Participants

4 of 5 target participants recruited outside the planned SME panel after handpicked recruitment stalled. Sample included 1 SME-panel (handpicked), 2 Gainsight in-app recruits, and 1 product-team personal contact.
