Summary

What we were right, wrong, and surprised about

Mapped against the six HMW statements and three risk areas (findability, comprehension, workflow disruption) from the test plan.

✓

What we were right about

  • Unified summary page is the right concept 7/7
  • Award Details bento is instantly findable 7/7
  • Hero Card stage tracker communicates status 6/7
  • Application access from Request Details works 6/7
  • Actions dropdown is findable for admin tasks 5/7
  • Slide-over is the preferred form view pattern 4/7 explicit, 0 negative
✗

What we were wrong about

  • Due dates are critical and missing 5/7 asked
  • Page doesn't self-identify as a request record 4/7 misidentified
  • Action buttons need next-step context, not just labels 2/7 + design principle
!

What surprised us

  • Layout rearrangement vs. consistency is a genuine participant disagreement
  • Multi-year grants may need different information hierarchy
  • Relationship-based grants may need different information hierarchy (e.g. more prominent contact card)
Key findings

The evidence behind the top findings

Each finding includes participant quotes and video clips where available. Clips can be played inline.

Convergent (4/7)

The page doesn't identify itself as a request record

Four participants initially described the page as an "organization page" or "organization summary" before self-correcting. The page relies on implicit cues (project title, stage tracker) to communicate record type, but there is no explicit label like "Grant Request" near the title.

This is the most actionable comprehension finding. Adding a record type indicator near the page title would resolve it.

"This appears to be an organization summary page... Not sure what this is. Is this one specific request? Or I guess this maybe isn't an organization, it's a request summary page."

Alex Hall

Convergent (5/7)

Due dates are the #1 missing data element

Five participants independently flagged the absence of dates. This was the strongest feedback signal in the study. Participants said date visibility is the primary reason they visit an individual request page, and without it they can't answer common questions from nonprofits or brief colleagues on timeline.

The data exists in the system. Submission dates should appear on Request Details rows, due dates on Follow Up rows, and overdue items need visual differentiation.

"Where are the dates for anything? The one thing we would actually go to the individual request versus looking at the workflow page is to know their specific due dates."

Caitlin Stanley

Emerging (2/7)

Action buttons need scope and context

The contextual action bar tells users what actions are available, but not why they're being surfaced or what they apply to. Will saw "Follow Up Complete" in the header and asked which of the three listed follow-ups it would affect. Separately, analysis revealed that "Assign/View Evaluators" does double duty as both a status indicator and an action trigger without clarifying which role it's playing.

Design principle: contextual buttons should communicate the next step, not just the available action.

"If I click follow-up complete, which of the multiple follow-ups is going to that pertain to? There are multiple follow-ups down here. I'm not sure what clicking either of these buttons would do."

Will Hayden

Validated (4/7)

Slide-over is the clear winner for form viewing

Four participants explicitly preferred slide-over after comparing all three patterns during free exploration. No participant preferred full replace or floating. The slide-over pattern keeps users in context while providing quick access to form content, which aligns with real workflows where grants managers reference applications without wanting to leave the summary page.

"The slide-over is by far my favorite. It's really nice."

Alex Hall
▶
Clip pending: Alex Hall Compares all three form view patterns and picks slide-over.
Open question

Should the layout adapt by stage, or stay consistent?

Participants genuinely disagree. Elise and Alex argued that Request Details should be demoted during Follow Up stage because grants managers spend months or years in that phase and rarely need historical application data front and center. But Caitlin explicitly pushed back: she doesn't want boxes to disappear because learning multiple versions of the same page is worse than knowing where everything always is.

Will's feedback suggests a middle path: keep all bentos in the same positions but use visual weight (icons, size, color accents) to draw attention to what's most relevant at each stage. This achieves stage-appropriate emphasis without breaking spatial memory.

This tension is the most important open design question heading into the next iteration.

"I don't wanna see boxes disappear. That's more confusing if you have to get used to multiple versions of the same page at different stages."

Caitlin Stanley

"I don't think I would need this request details this visible... this could be minimized. The only items useful to me halfway through an award are follow-ups and award details."

Elise
Recommendations

What to do next

Ship with confidence

  • Expanded layout with all bentos visible
  • Slide-over as the form viewing pattern
  • Hero Card with color-coded stage tracker
  • Award Details bento structure
  • Actions dropdown for admin tasks
  • Request Details bento for form/eval access

Iterate before shipping

  • Add due dates to Request Details rows and Follow Up rows. Add overdue visual indicators. (5/7)
  • Add record type label near the page title to differentiate from org records. (4/7)
  • Scope Follow Up Complete to clarify which follow-up it applies to.
  • Add next-step context to action buttons (e.g., "LOI reviewed. Assign the next form.")

Test in a follow-up round

  • Visual hierarchy variations (icons, weighted sizing) as a middle path for stage emphasis
  • Clickable Hero Card stage nodes as form shortcuts
  • Sidebar scroll-to-bento behavior
  • Overdue status visual treatment
  • Contact card prominence for relationship-based contexts

Data pulls to inform next steps

  • Multi-year grants: How many active clients have grants with 24+ month durations? Helps anticipate where feedback will cluster.
  • Relationship-based grantmaking: How many clients would characterize their approach this way? Informs contact card priority.
Reference

Study design

Unmoderated remote usability study conducted through Userlytics. Seven GLM clients completed the study against a target of 10. Participants saw three locked prototype states (Follow Up Submitted, Decision Approved, LOI Submitted) plus a free exploration mode. All form viewing was locked to Pattern B (slide-over). The prototype (v3.3) included contextual action buttons.

Tasks traced back to six HMW statements and three risk areas (findability, comprehension, workflow disruption). Several write-in tasks were omitted to stay within the 30-minute cap; verbal video responses were prioritized instead.

Confidence thresholds (adjusted for n=7)

  • Convergent (5-7 of 7): Act on now.
  • Emerging (3-4 of 7): Document and watch.
  • Isolated (1-2 of 7): Note, don't act yet.

Limitations

  • 7 of 10 target participants. Emerging signals carry lower confidence.
  • No difficulty rating scales or SUS scores collected (omitted for time).
  • Role segmentation not possible at n=7.
  • Seeded dummy data didn't match all participants' real configurations (no LOI process, more complex budgets, more evaluators).
  • Several participants experienced friction with the Userlytics platform interface (advancing between activities, overlay windows). Some hesitation was platform-driven, not prototype-driven.

Participants

Elise, Patti Biera, Alex Hall, Aamina Ahmed, Elisabeth Jackson, Will Hayden, Caitlin Stanley. Recruited via Gainsight in-app link. No intentional segment split.

Follow-up action buttons expected near the Follow Ups bento (2/7)

Alex's eye naturally went to the Follow Ups bento for follow-up actions. Patti wanted assignee names inline within follow-up rows. Pre-existing hypothesis confirmed by testing.

Hero Card stage nodes expected to be clickable (1/7)

Will tried to click stage tracker nodes to navigate into forms. Isolated signal but an interesting affordance question.

Sidebar nav items don't scroll to bentos (2/7)

Caitlin and Will noted clicking sidebar items didn't visibly do anything. Will suggested matching icons between sidebar and bento headers.

Same badge color for different statuses (1/7)

Caitlin noted Submitted and Assigned both use info-blue, undermining color coding value.

Requested amount irrelevant post-approval (1/7)

Will said he'd prefer the Hero Card to show Paid vs Balance instead of Requested vs Awarded after approval.

Financials and Compliance may not need a full bento (1/7)

Will suggested compliance status could be represented as compact icons near the project name rather than a dedicated module.

Mobile responsiveness (1/7)

Alex noted they "do lots of things on the go" and was curious how the layout would stack on mobile.
